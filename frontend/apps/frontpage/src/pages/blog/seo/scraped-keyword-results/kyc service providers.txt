KYC (Know Your Customer) technology is a set of processes and tools employed by financial institutions and businesses to verify the identity of their customers. It involves collecting and analyzing customer information to ensure that they are who they claim to be. ​ KYC processes typically include the collection of personal information, document verification (e.g., passports, driver's licenses), biometric authentication, and screening against global sanctions lists and watchlists. The goal of KYC technology is to establish a customer's identity and assess their risk profile accurately. The KYC framework contains four steps: customer identification program (CIP), customer due diligence (CDD), enhanced due diligence (EDD) and ongoing monitoring. 1. Customer Identification Program (CIP): ​ The Customer Identification Program is the initial step in KYC. It focuses on collecting and verifying customer information to establish their identity. Key aspects of CIP include: ​ Gathering basic customer information, such as name, date of birth, address, and contact details. ​ Requesting official identification documents, such as passports, driver's licenses, or national ID cards, to validate the customer's identity.​ ​ Conducting basic background checks and screenings to ensure that the provided information is accurate and matches official records. ​ 2. Customer Due Diligence (CDD): ​ Customer Due Diligence is the second step in the KYC process, following CIP. It involves a more comprehensive assessment of the customer's risk profile and financial activities. The main objectives of CDD are: ​ Evaluating the customer's transaction history and patterns. ​ Understanding the source of funds and wealth associated with the customer's accounts. ​ Identifying any politically exposed persons (PEPs) or high-risk entities with whom the customer is associated. ​ Categorizing the customer's risk level as low, medium, or high based on the gathered information. ​ 3. Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD): ​ Enhanced Due Diligence is an additional step in the KYC process that is reserved for customers deemed to be high risk based on the results of CDD. EDD involves a more in-depth investigation into the customer's background, financial activities, and associations. Specific aspects of EDD include: ​ Conducting a thorough review of the source of funds and business relationships. ​ Understanding the source of funds and wealth associated with the customer's accounts. ​ Identifying any politically exposed persons (PEPs) or high-risk entities with whom the customer is associated. ​ Categorizing the customer's risk level as low, medium, or high based on the gathered information. ​ 4. Ongoing Monitoring: ​ Ongoing Monitoring is the final step in the KYC process and is a continuous effort throughout the customer relationship. Its primary objectives are: ​ Continuously monitoring customer accounts and transactions for any suspicious or unusual activities. ​ Updating customer information and risk assessments on a regular basis to reflect any changes. ​ Reporting and investigating any red flags or suspicious activities promptly. ​ Ensuring ongoing compliance with regulatory requirements and making necessary adjustments to mitigate emerging risks. ​ These four steps collectively form a comprehensive KYC process that helps financial institutions and businesses verify the identities of their customers, assess their risk levels, and mitigate the potential for financial crimes such as money laundering and fraud. ​ The KYC framework is not a one-time process but rather a continuous cycle. It is essential for mitigating risks related to money laundering, fraud, terrorist financing, and other financial crimes. By following these three steps, financial institutions and businesses can maintain a comprehensive understanding of their customers, prevent illicit activities, and comply with regulatory requirements. Role of KYC in the Fintech industry KYC regulations have their roots in a history marked by uncontrolled financial misconduct. The initial directives were formulated back in 1970, caused by the adoption of the US Bank Secrecy Act (BSA), the main purpose of which is to combat money laundering. Subsequent significant additions followed later after the September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks and the global financial crisis of 2008. ​ The role of KYC is pivotal in various industries, particularly in the financial sector. Today KYC and related Anti-Money Laundering (AML) measures are essential for fintech firms as integral components of their compliance protocols. These companies must guarantee their adherence to both local and, where relevant, international rules governing the process of verifying customer identities. KYC processes help prevent a range of problems and risks associated with identity fraud, money laundering, terrorist financing, and other financial crimes. ​ Here's an overview of the role of KYC and the problems it can prevent: ​ Identity Verification: KYC ensures that financial institutions and businesses verify the identity of their customers. This helps prevent impersonation, identity theft, and the use of fraudulent identities for various purposes, including opening accounts and conducting transactions. ​ Fraud Prevention: KYC measures play a crucial role in fraud prevention. By verifying customer identities and scrutinizing their financial activities, businesses can detect and prevent fraudulent transactions, such as unauthorized account access or fraudulent fund transfers. ​ Money Laundering Prevention: KYC is a vital component of Anti-Money Laundering (AML) efforts. It helps identify and deter money laundering activities where illicit funds are disguised as legitimate assets. By scrutinizing customer transactions and behavior, suspicious activities can be flagged and reported to regulatory authorities. ​ Compliance with Regulatory Requirements: Financial institutions are legally obligated to comply with KYC regulations imposed by local and international authorities. Failure to do so can result in severe penalties and damage to a business's reputation. KYC ensures compliance with these regulations. ​ Risk Assessment: KYC helps assess the risk profile of customers. By categorizing customers as low, medium, or high risk, businesses can allocate resources and apply appropriate due diligence measures to mitigate risks effectively. ​ Preventing Unauthorized Transactions: KYC helps ensure that transactions are conducted by authorized individuals. This reduces the risk of unauthorized access to accounts and financial assets. ​ Data Security: KYC processes also contribute to data security by ensuring that sensitive customer information is handled with care and that it doesn't fall into the wrong hands. ​ In summary, KYC plays a vital role in safeguarding the integrity of financial systems, preventing financial crimes, protecting businesses from regulatory violations, and enhancing overall security and trust in the financial industry and beyond. Let's start with a definition of KYC and eKYC and discover how advanced ID verification systems can better support KYC processes . Ready? What is KYC? KYC means Know Your Customer and sometimes Know Your Client. KYC or KYC check is the mandatory process of identifying and verifying the client's identity when opening an account and periodically over time. In other words, banks must ensure that their clients are genuinely who they claim to be. Banks may refuse to open an account or halt a business relationship if the client fails to meet minimum KYC requirements. KYC procedures defined by banks involve all the necessary actions to ensure their customers are real and assess and monitor risks. These client-onboarding processes help prevent and identify money laundering, terrorism financing, and other illegal corruption schemes. KYC process includes ID card verification, face verification, document verification such as utility bills as proof of address, and biometric verification. Banks must comply with KYC regulations and anti-money laundering regulations to limit fraud. KYC compliance responsibility rests with the banks. In case of failure to comply, heavy penalties can be applied. In the U.S., Europe, the Middle East, and the Asia Pacific, a cumulated USD26 billion in fines have been levied for non-compliance with AML, KYC, and sanctions fines the past ten years (2008-2018) - let alone the reputational damage done and not measured. According to the United Nations, criminals are laundering between $1.6 to $4 trillion annually (2 to 5% of global GDP). Stricter KYC/CDD processes are helping to stop that. KYC documents KYC checks are done through an independent and reliable source of documents, data, or information. Each client is required to provide credentials to prove identity and address .
