Anti-Money Laundering (AML) compliance refers to the policies, procedures, and regulations designed to prevent, detect, and report money laundering activities. It aims to curb illegal practices, such as funneling illicitly-gained funds through legitimate financial systems to disguise their criminal origins, thereby preserving financial market integrity.

AML compliance is crucial for safeguarding the financial system from crimes. It helps to prevent money laundering, terrorist financing, and other forms of illicit activity by identifying and addressing various risks.

What are the Key Components of an AML Compliance Program?

An AML compliance program should include:

  1. Written policies and procedures
  2. Designation of a compliance officer
  3. Ongoing employee training
  4. Independent review to monitor and ensure AML program is adequately functioning
  5. Customer identification program (CIP)
  6. Risk-based customer due diligence (CDD)
  7. Ongoing transaction monitoring
  8. Reporting of suspicious activity

What is the Role of the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) in AML Compliance?

FinCEN is a US government agency responsible for administering and enforcing the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) and other AML laws. FinCEN provides guidance and regulations to help financial institutions comply with AML requirements and also collects and analyzes data to identify and track money laundering and other financial crimes.

Footprint's platform can help financial institutions meet FinCEN's requirements by providing accurate and robust identity verification.


What is the Difference Between AML and KYC (Know Your Customer)?

AML refers to the broader set of policies and procedures designed to prevent money laundering, while KYC is a specific component of AML that involves verifying the identity of customers and assessing their risk profile.

What is Customer Due Diligence (CDD)?

CDD is the process of gathering and verifying information about a customer to assess their risk profile and ensure that they are not involved in money laundering or other illicit activities.

What is Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)?

EDD is a more thorough and detailed process of gathering and verifying information about a customer, typically used for higher-risk customers or those that are subject to stricter AML regulations.

Footprint's platform can support EDD by providing a comprehensive and accurate picture of a customer's identity and risk profile.
